# VURT Audience — Psychographics, Not Demographics

You will never see "Black women 25-44" or "Gen Z males in Tier 1 cities" in a VURT brief. Demographic targeting is the wrong shape for a culture-first platform. Cultures cross demographics; demographics don't predict culture.

---

## The Five Cultures (current)

From the master doc and culture-tags reference. Each is massive. Each is voracious for content. None has a platform home.

### Hood / Hip-Hop Culture
- **Audience:** People who organize their identity, language, fashion, and music around hip-hop and street culture. Spans race, region, age.
- **What they want from a platform:** Stories told from inside the culture, not about it. Producers and casts who came up. Music supervision that knows. No respectability filter.
- **Active VURT shows:** Karma in Heels, Chief Keef / Glo Navy.
- **Voice cues for copy:** Dialogue-led headlines. Verbatim show language. Peer-voice on social. Never "urban." Never "Black cinema."

### Faith / Family-Legacy Culture
- **Audience:** People who organize around generational, religious, and family-legacy stakes. Often Black church / Caribbean / Latino multi-generational households.
- **What they want from a platform:** Stories about generational consequence, family secrets, redemption arcs that respect the faith without preaching.
- **Active VURT shows:** Come Back Dad, The Parking Lot Series.
- **Voice cues:** Stakes-as-surprise framing ("She wasn't supposed to find out like this"). Family reveals. Inheritance/blood/legacy language.

### Romance / Drama Culture
- **Audience:** Voracious daily-consumption audience that drives ReelShort and DramaBox numbers. Mostly female-presenting, all ages. Pays attention to chemistry, conflict, slow-burn.
- **What they want from a platform:** Production quality + cultural specificity (NOT the generic European-knockoff aesthetic that dominates DramaBox). Open-loop endings.
- **Active VURT shows:** Karma in Heels romance arcs, Original drops in dev.
- **Voice cues:** Question-form open loops. Character-voice teases. Slow-burn promises.

### Gaming Culture
- **Audience:** 3.24B globally. Gen Z + younger millennials. Lives on Twitch, Discord, YouTube. Already comfortable with vertical and short-form.
- **What they want from a platform:** Story content that respects gaming as culture (lore, in-game world-building, esports drama), not "content for gamers" condescension.
- **Active VURT shows:** In dev. Slate is being built.
- **Voice cues:** Lore-specific language. Insider terminology. Avoid using "gamer" as a noun in copy.

### Horror Culture
- **Audience:** 84% of US 13+ consume horror content. Year-round, not just October. Strong culture-of-discovery (Reddit, YouTube horror essayists, A24 fandom).
- **What they want from a platform:** Production quality, slow dread, smart genre work. Hates jump-scare-only.
- **Active VURT shows:** Slate in dev.
- **Voice cues:** Restraint. Negative space. Single object on flat field (the A24 horror playbook).

## Cross-cultural behavior

The same audience belongs to multiple cultures. A 28-year-old woman in Atlanta might be:
- Daily romance-drama viewer
- Faith-rooted family-legacy viewer
- Casual horror viewer (October peaks)
- Hip-hop culture native

Your concept does not need to address all five at once. It needs to address ONE with conviction. Range is shown across the campaign rotation, not within a single piece.

---

## What this audience pays attention to

From research and Dioni's stated read:

- **Specificity over breadth.** A line written for one culture lands harder than a line trying to cover three.
- **Production craft as respect.** Bad audio reads as "they don't care about us."
- **Insider language as belonging signal.** AAVE used wrong = instant flag. Used right = home.
- **Community recommendation over algorithm.** "My cousin sent me this" > "the FYP showed me."
- **Anti-cringe filter is on at all times.** Em dashes, exclamation marks, performance, generic tags = swiped away.

---

## What this audience does NOT do

- Read app store listings before downloading.
- Type long URLs from memory.
- Click "Sign up to keep watching" walls without reservation.
- Engage with brand-voice posts that don't reference the show.
- Forgive cultural performance from a brand that hasn't earned it.

---

## How this changes the copy

Once you know which culture(s) the concept is talking to:

- Drop demographic language.
- Use culture-specific in-world references.
- Pick ONE voice mode (in-world / out-world / peer).
- Test the line against the question: **would someone INSIDE this culture screenshot this and send it to a friend?**

If yes, you're in. If no, the work is for the wrong audience or in the wrong voice.

---

## The "phantom audience" trap

The mediocre-CD failure: writing for an imaginary "general audience" that doesn't exist. There is no general audience. There are only specific cultures. Pick one. Commit.

If you find yourself softening language to "be inclusive of multiple audiences," you've already written generic. Better to write five sharp culture-specific concepts than one bland multi-culture one.
