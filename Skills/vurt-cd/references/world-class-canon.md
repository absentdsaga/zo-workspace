# World-Class Canon — Benchmarks, Commandments, Mistakes

The canon you reference. The campaigns you study. The principles you apply. Specific, named, verifiable.

---

## The 10 Commandments of 0.01% Creative Direction

### 1. Lead with the wound, not the win
Nike's 1997 "Failure" Jordan ad — Michael Jordan in a tunnel: *"I've missed more than 9000 shots in my career. I've lost almost 300 games. 26 times I've been trusted to take the game-winning shot…and missed. I've failed over and over and over again in my life. And that is why I succeed."* Brand never appears as savior — only silent witness to a god admitting he's fallible.

**Mediocre would have:** Cut a sizzle reel of dunks with the swoosh on the corner.

### 2. Tell the customer not to buy
Patagonia's full-page New York Times ad, Black Friday 2011: **"DON'T BUY THIS JACKET"** over a hero shot of their bestselling R2 fleece, then itemized 135 liters of water and 20 lbs of CO2. Closed: *"Don't buy what you don't need. Think twice before you buy anything."*

**Mediocre would have:** Run a "20% OFF" banner with a green leaf icon.

### 3. Frame the brand as a worldview, not a product
Apple "Think Different" (TBWA\Chiat\Day, 1997, narrated by Richard Dreyfuss) — never showed a computer: *"Here's to the crazy ones. The misfits. The rebels. The troublemakers. The round pegs in the square holes."* Product is the price of admission to a club.

**Mediocre would have:** Shown the Mac and listed RAM specs.

### 4. Weaponize the flaw
Volkswagen's 1960 "Lemon" ad (DDB, Julian Koenig + Helmut Krone) — a Beetle with a tiny photo and a single word: **"Lemon."** Body explained the car was rejected at final inspection because *"the chrome strip on the glove compartment is blemished and must be replaced."* Closed: *"We pluck the lemons; you get the plums."*

**Mediocre would have:** Airbrushed the chrome and shot at sunset.

### 5. Embrace your rank
Avis (DDB, Paula Green, 1962): **"When you're only No. 2, you try harder. Or else."**

**Mediocre would have:** Claimed parity with Hertz.

### 6. Apologize the way only you would speak
KFC UK ran out of chicken, Feb 2018. Full-page ad in The Sun and Metro: empty bucket relabeled **"FCK."** Body: *"A chicken restaurant without any chicken. It's not ideal. Huge apologies to our customers, especially those who travelled out of their way to find we were closed."*

**Mediocre would have:** Issued a corporate-passive press release.

### 7. Let the world become the medium
Apple "Shot on iPhone" turned customer photos plastered on building-sized billboards into both the product demo AND the campaign. Squid Game pushed the same idea: a giant pink piggy bank in Sydney's Pitt Street Mall, pink Triangle/Circle/Square guards riding the Paris Metro and Seoul subway. The IP escapes the screen.

**Mediocre would have:** Bought 30s pre-roll on YouTube.

### 8. Make the typography do the heavy lifting
Off-White's quotation marks ("SHOELACES", "FOR WALKING ON", "LITTLE BLACK DRESS") demystify the object and create the brand simultaneously. Virgil Abloh: *"You can use typography and wording to completely change the perception of a thing without changing anything about it."* Netflix's Stranger Things and Wednesday OOH frequently runs as nothing but the wordmark in the show's typeface.

**Mediocre would have:** Crammed four faces, a network logo, and a release date into one frame.

### 9. Build a voice consistent enough that one line does the whole job
Liquid Death — **"Murder Your Thirst."** Old Spice — *"Hello, ladies. Look at your man, now back to me, now back to your man, now back to me."* Glossier — *"Skin first. Makeup second. Smile always."* You can identify the brand from one sentence with no logo.

**Mediocre would have:** Written copy that could be rebadged for any competitor.

### 10. Stage the absurd as if it were a service
Cards Against Humanity "Holiday Hole" (Black Friday 2016): livestreamed a backhoe digging a hole in Illinois with the running line *"As long as money keeps coming in, we'll keep digging."* The promise is anti-promise. They made $100,573.

**Mediocre would have:** Run "30% off all packs."

## The 7 Mediocre-CD Mistakes

### 1. The sizzle-reel reflex
Cutting fast clips to a needle-drop song instead of finding a single pressure point.
**Symptom:** A montage that could be any show, any sport, any product.

### 2. The ensemble cram
Putting all six lead actors' faces on the OOH because nobody wants to fight about which one to feature.
**Symptom:** A billboard at 65 mph reads as a blurry crowd; no one remembers a single face.

### 3. Performing "edgy" without committing
Borrowing the gesture (skull icon, swear word, glitch type) but softening the verb.
**Symptom:** Tagline reads "Disrupt Your Thirst" instead of "Murder Your Thirst."

### 4. Burying the hero in chrome
Logo lockups, network bugs, sponsor footers, QR codes, release dates, hashtags, age ratings — all eating 40% of the canvas.
**Symptom:** Can't tell what the campaign is FOR in 3 seconds.

### 5. Speaking corporate-third-person
"We are excited to announce…" "Our brand stands for…"
**Symptom:** Copy is grammatically perfect and emotionally inert; could have been written by legal.

### 6. Confusing a theme for an idea
"Empowerment," "Connection," "Belonging" — these are categories, not ideas. An idea has a verb and a wound.
**Symptom:** Deck has a one-word title and a stock-photo mood board.

### 7. Optimizing the wrong metric
Designing for the agency reel or the awards jury instead of the street.
**Symptom:** Wins a Bronze Lion. Nobody on the street can quote a single line.

## Self-Critique Heuristics — ask before declaring done

1. **Cover the logo. Is the brand still obvious?** If no, the voice isn't strong enough.
2. **Read the line out loud. Does it sound like a human or a press release?**
3. **Strip every word that could appear in a competitor's ad. What's left?**
4. **What is the audience supposed to FEEL in the first 1.5 seconds — not understand, feel?**
5. **Where's the wound, the risk, the sacrifice?** If everything is upside, nobody believes it.
6. **Could a 7-year-old retell this campaign at dinner?** If not, it's too clever.
7. **Will this look dated in 18 months?** Trend-typography and meme references age in weeks.
8. **What did I leave OUT that took courage?** (The chrome strip on Lemon. The "Don't" in Don't Buy This Jacket. The empty bucket in FCK.)
9. **If we ran this with no media spend, would anyone share it?** If no, we're renting attention, not earning it.
10. **Is there a single object, line, or image a fan would tattoo?**

## Entertainment IP OOH Specifically

### 1. One image, one line, no chrome
HBO's *"It's Not TV. It's HBO."* ran for two decades attached to teaser art with zero cast lockups. Sopranos final season (Annie Leibovitz) used *"Hell hath no fury like the family"* over a Renaissance dynasty portrait — one image, one line.

### 2. Show props before faces
AMC's Better Call Saul colonized Albuquerque with Jimmy McGill's universe — the giant inflatable Statue of Liberty, courthouse benches reading *"Better Call Saul!"* — rather than putting Bob Odenkirk's headshot on a vinyl. The world is the star.

### 3. Let the IP escape the frame
A24 left handmade Charlie dolls (from *Hereditary*) on doorsteps of audience members the night after midnight screenings — no copy, no logo, just the artifact. *Everything Everywhere All at Once* dressed buildings in googly eyes.

### 4. The location is the casting
Squid Game's pink-suited guards in transit hubs (Sydney piggy bank, Seoul/Paris metros) — the costume on a real escalator does what a billboard cannot.

### 5. Typography as costume
Stranger Things' ITC Benguiat in red on black needs no character art. Wednesday's gothic display face spread one letter per poster across an NYC building.

### 6. Use silence and white space as menace
A24 horror posters routinely run a single object on a flat color field (the dollhouse in *Hereditary*; the tree in *The Witch*). No copy except the title.

### 7. Speak in the show's register
Netflix's "Spoiler Billboard" (Peru, 2017) actually spoiled hit shows on outdoor placements unless people switched to Netflix — the prank IS the brand voice.

## In-World vs. Out-World — when each works

**Out-World** — when the brand has cultural authority and a distinct voice. *"It's Not TV. It's HBO."* Mubi's curator-essayist voice. Spotify Wrapped speaking AS Spotify. Criterion blurbs in cinephile-essayist register. **Use when:** brand is tastemaker, catalog is broad, halo across many titles.

**In-World** — when the IP is the engine. KFC FCK ad. A24 Charlie dolls. Squid Game pink soldiers (no Netflix N's). Stranger Things billboards (no "Netflix Original"). Off-White "SHOELACES" (no "by Virgil Abloh"). **Use when:** IP iconography is stronger than parent brand, audience wants to play inside the fiction, parent logo would break the spell.

The masters know which mode they're in BEFORE the first sketch. Mediocre CDs default to the parent brand always being on-screen because someone in marketing said so.
