# Playbook — Concept Development

Most mediocre creative work is born here, in the gap between "we need a concept for X" and "here is the concept." Themes get presented as ideas. Decoration gets presented as direction. This playbook closes that gap.

---

## What a concept is (and isn't)

A **theme** is a noun: empowerment, connection, belonging, vertical cinema, free, culture.
A **concept** is a sentence with a verb and a wound: *"Your culture had nowhere to live. We built it a home."*

If your output is one or two words, it's a theme, not a concept. Keep working.

---

## The 5-question concept brief

Before you sketch, answer all five. Out loud. To yourself. Don't skip.

### 1. Whose stake?

Whose problem does this concept solve in 1.5 seconds?
- VURT's stake → out-world voice
- The show's stake → in-world voice
- The audience's stake → peer voice

Pick one. Commit.

### 2. What's the wound?

What absence does this concept name? What is the audience missing right now that VURT addresses?

Mediocre: "VURT offers vertical cinema for cultures."
Concept: "Your culture had nowhere to live."

### 3. What's the asymmetric stake?

What's the risk, refusal, or asymmetric scarcity that makes this concept interesting?

Mediocre: "Watch free."
Concept: "Always free to watch. Forever. Even when we're profitable."

### 4. What's the one thing that survives a 1.5-second glance?

If they only see one image, one line, one feeling — what is it?

Mediocre: "The whole composition working together."
Concept: "The line *'I told you not to come back.'*"

### 5. What's left out that took courage?

What did you NOT include that another CD would have crammed in?

Mediocre: "Everything fits."
Concept: "No genre tag. No release date. No app-store badge. Just the line."

## The "What's it about?" elevator test

You should be able to explain the concept to a stranger in two sentences:

> *"This concept says X about VURT, by doing Y, in the voice of Z."*

If it takes a deck to explain, the concept is a theme.

Examples:

- ✅ *"This concept says VURT is a home for cultures, by inviting industry-only attendees to a specific rooftop on a specific night, in VURT's own voice."* (= C1-E)
- ✅ *"This concept says you can come back to a story that knows you, by speaking the show's verbatim dialogue with no streaming-app chrome around it, in the show's voice."* (= a hypothetical Karma in Heels in-world poster)
- ❌ *"This concept shows that VURT is for everyone, by featuring multiple cultures and the value prop, in a fun, energetic voice."* (= a theme deck disguised as a concept)

---

## The "single asset" test

Strip the campaign down to a single asset. If it can survive as one piece — one billboard, one poster, one tweet — the concept is real. If it needs the whole rollout to make sense, the concept is decoration.

Apple's "1984" lived on one Super Bowl spot. Patagonia's "DON'T BUY THIS JACKET" lived on one full-page ad. Cards Against Humanity's Holiday Hole lived on one livestream. Each piece carried the whole idea.

---

## The "one line" test

Can you describe the concept in one sentence the team can repeat without paraphrasing?

- ✅ "Names alone don't carry FOMO. We use 'BY INVITATION' as the headline and the names as proof."
- ❌ "We're going to do something with the cofounders' names and maybe a date and the venue."

The first one is a concept. The second is a list.

---

## The "what's the second draft?" trap

You write a concept. It's pretty good. You feel done. STOP. Write a SECOND concept that disagrees with the first. Then a third.

Most mediocre work is the first idea, polished. The work that lasts is the third or fourth idea, after you've thrown out the obvious ones.

For OOH, this means writing 3-5 directions per brief. For taglines, 10-20. For show-launch creative, 5-7. The volume is not a waste; it's the system.

---

## The "kill your sizzle" rule

If your concept includes a montage of clips, a sweeping needle-drop song, a fast-cut sizzle, or "we'll show all the shows" energy — STOP.

That's the sizzle-reel reflex. It's the most common mediocre move. Replace it with one image, one line, one feeling.

---

## When you have multiple concepts

Present 3-5 directions. For each, fill in:

```
## [Concept name]
**Stake:** Whose stake (VURT / show / audience)
**Voice:** Out-world / in-world / peer
**Mechanism:** FOMO / single-hero / IP-escape / negative-pull / specific-receipt
**Wound:** What absence is named
**One-line ID:** The line, image, or feeling that survives a 1.5s glance
**Courage cut:** What's deliberately NOT included
**Reference benchmark:** Which world-class campaign this echoes
```

Then run all four critic subagents on each. Cull to one. Refine. Ship.

---

## What you don't do

- Present a "mood board" without copy.
- Offer "two directions" that are the same idea in two color palettes.
- Use a stock photo as a stand-in for an idea.
- Pitch "exploration phase" without a recommendation.
- Suggest a campaign without naming the mechanism.
- Use "engagement" as a goal — engagement is a metric, not a goal.

---

## What you DO

- Present concepts that disagree with each other.
- Include the courage cut explicitly.
- Show the reference benchmark.
- Recommend one. Stand behind it.
- Name what would make this concept fail (so it can be tested).
