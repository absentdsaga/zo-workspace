# In-World vs. Out-World vs. Peer Voice

Three distinct voices. Mixing them accidentally is the #1 cause of VURT creative reading like mush. Pick BEFORE sketching.

---

## Out-World Voice — VURT speaks as itself

VURT is the protagonist. The brand has cultural authority and is making a statement.

**When to use:**
- Brand-level OOH (taglines, brand awareness boards)
- Launch announcements
- Press / industry communications
- Site copy, app store listing, deck headlines
- The "Always-On" panel of any campaign

**Voice characteristics:**
- "Built for the Culture."
- "Vertical Cinema."
- "Always free to watch."
- Confident, declarative, low-emoji, no caps-lock except hero phrases.

**Reference benchmarks:**
- HBO: "It's Not TV. It's HBO." (the brand is the protagonist)
- Mubi: curator-essayist voice across every title
- Spotify Wrapped: platform speaks as itself

**Failure mode:** When in out-world voice, do NOT pretend to be inside the show. The brand speaks for itself and points at the show.

---

## In-World Voice — the show speaks, VURT disappears

The IP carries the work. VURT is the doorman, not the host. The audience plays inside the fiction.

**When to use:**
- Show-launch OOH where the show's iconography is strong
- Clip captions where dialogue is the hook
- Premiere events for a specific show
- Posters / one-sheets for a single Original
- Phone-frame mockups (when justified by format and audience)

**Voice characteristics:**
- Verbatim character lines: "I told you not to come back."
- Show-prop iconography (Better Call Saul's tacky business cards, A24's Charlie dolls)
- Show-typeface (Stranger Things ITC Benguiat, Wednesday gothic)
- VURT logo present but small / subordinate

**Reference benchmarks:**
- A24's Hereditary Charlie dolls on doorsteps (no A24 mark visible)
- Squid Game's pink soldiers in subway stations (no Netflix N's)
- Stranger Things' Hawkins billboards (typeface only, no "Netflix Original")
- Better Call Saul's Albuquerque takeover (Jimmy McGill's universe, not AMC's)

**Failure mode:** When in in-world voice, do NOT cap the work with a library-genre tag ("Vertical Cinema · Free"). That breaks the fiction. The viewer was just inside Karma in Heels' world; the genre tag yanks them back to the streaming-app shelf.

---

## Peer Voice — the audience speaks

The most niche and most powerful. The work sounds like it was written BY a fan, FOR a fan, ABOUT the show. The brand recedes behind the community.

**When to use:**
- Pinned comments on TikTok / IG
- "Reaction" carousel slides (e.g. "Y'all saw this coming?" / "She really did that")
- Community / user-generated re-post creative
- Insider-language headlines for highly culture-fluent shows
- Quote-card formats where a character line drives engagement

**Voice characteristics:**
- "Tell me she's not about to…"
- "Y'all weren't ready for this."
- "Send this to the friend who…"
- AAVE / regional vernacular when authentic to the show's culture (never performed)
- Lowercase, casual punctuation

**Reference benchmarks:**
- Old Spice's "Hello, ladies" (peer-mode flirting)
- Liquid Death's product copy ("infinitely recyclable cans of premium low-calorie beverages will absolutely murder your thirst") — peer-tone written from inside the metal/punk subculture
- Glossier's early IG comments — sounds like a friend, not a brand

**Failure mode:** When in peer voice, do NOT say things a real fan wouldn't say. "OMG y'all the new episode is FIRE 🔥🔥" is performed. "she really walked right back into that house knowing what was waiting" is peer.

---

## How to pick

Ask three questions in order:

1. **Whose stake is this concept selling?**
   - VURT's stake (brand range, free-to-watch, culture-home positioning) → **out-world**
   - The show's stake (this scene, this character, this episode) → **in-world**
   - The community's stake (the reaction, the in-group, the inside joke) → **peer**

2. **Whose name is on the work?**
   - Logo prominent → **out-world**
   - Logo small or absent → **in-world**
   - Logo absent, account-level branding only (e.g. @myvurt account) → **peer**

3. **What does the audience do after seeing it?**
   - Remembers VURT exists → **out-world**
   - Wants to watch the specific show → **in-world**
   - Tags a friend / quotes the line / re-posts → **peer**

Pick one. Commit. Don't drift.

---

## The most common mixing error

**Out-world tagline appended to in-world headline.**

Example: V2-D Phone Hijack mockup. The in-world dialogue caption "I told you not to come back." was correct in-world voice. The label underneath — "Karma in Heels · S1·E3 / Vertical Cinema · Free" — was out-world category-explainer voice. The two voices clashed. The viewer was just inside the show, then got reminded they were on a streaming-app shelf.

**Fix:** Pick one mode. If in-world, drop the genre tag entirely. The dialogue is the work.

---

## Multi-mode campaigns

Some campaigns need multiple voices across slots. That's fine — but each slot picks ONE voice.

Example:
- C1-E (Opening Night billboard) → out-world: "BY INVITATION. ROOFTOP. 5.29." VURT is the protagonist; the rooftop is the offer.
- C2-G (Always-On billboard) → out-world: "Vertical Cinema. Built for the Culture. Always free to watch."
- A future show-launch poster for Karma in Heels → in-world: a single character image and "I told you not to come back." No genre tag, no "vertical cinema" framing.
- A pinned comment on the show's TikTok → peer: "Tell me she didn't really think she could come back."

Each piece of creative is one voice. The campaign as a whole shows range.
